Activity 1B · Fact Recall · Choose the best answer
1. What was "laissez-faire" economics, and what did the Great Depression do to it?
✓ Correct. "Laissez-faire" (French: "let it be") was the dominant economic philosophy before the Depression — the idea that free markets, left alone, would always find equilibrium. The Depression's 30% collapse of global output in three years made that philosophy untenable. The question shifted from whether governments should intervene to how.
2. What were the three goals of FDR's New Deal?
✓ Correct. The video summarizes the New Deal's three goals clearly: Relief — immediate help for those suffering; Recovery — government spending on public works to restart economic activity; Reform — structural changes to banks and new safety nets (like Social Security) so the next crisis wouldn't be as catastrophic. This was Keynesian theory in practice.
3. What made Germany's hyperinflation crisis a symbol of "total systemic failure"?
✓ Correct. The video uses this vivid image — currency so worthless it was used as wallpaper — to illustrate what total monetary collapse looks like. Germany's hyperinflation was caused by the crushing WWI reparations demanded by the Treaty of Versailles; unable to pay, Germany printed money, devaluing it into worthlessness. This wiped out the savings of an entire middle class, creating the conditions of desperation that would later fuel extremism.
4. What was Stalin's collectivization policy, and what was its human cost?
✓ Correct. Collectivization was central to Stalin's Five-Year Plans: seize all private farmland, force peasants onto massive state farms, control the food supply, sell grain abroad to fund industrialization. Peasants who resisted were killed or sent to gulags. The resulting disruption of food production caused deliberate famines — the Holodomor in Ukraine killed millions. The video calls it "industrialization through pure terror."
5. According to the video, what was the ONE thing all responses to the Depression had in common?
✓ Correct. This is the video's big takeaway — the "one massive world-changing outcome" that cuts across all responses. USA, fascist Italy, Mexico, Brazil, Soviet Union: despite totally different ideologies, they all ended up dramatically expanding government's economic role. Before the Depression, government responsibility for the economy was a radical idea. After it, the idea became a permanent global assumption.

Activity 2A · Explain Drill · Keynesian Economics
Prompt: Explain Keynesian economics and why it was considered a "complete game changer" compared to laissez-faire thinking. Use the New Deal as a specific example of Keynesian theory in practice.
Strong answer includes: Before Keynes, the dominant economic logic — laissez-faire — held that governments should stay out of markets and wait for them to self-correct. When the Depression hit and markets didn't correct (they collapsed), this advice looked catastrophically wrong. John Maynard Keynes offered a revolutionary alternative: in a crisis, he argued, the government must become the "spender of last resort." When private businesses stop investing and consumers stop spending — creating a downward spiral — only the government has the capacity to inject enough money into the economy to stop the freefall, even if it has to borrow to do it. This was the exact opposite of laissez-faire. The New Deal put this theory directly into practice: massive public works programs (building roads, dams, schools) created government-funded jobs; direct relief programs put money in people's hands to spend; and banking reforms rebuilt public trust in financial institutions. The government wasn't replacing the market — it was rescuing it.
Activity 2B · Explain Drill · Fascist Corporatism vs. Soviet Command Economy
Prompt: Both fascist Italy and the Soviet Union dramatically expanded state control of the economy during the interwar period. Explain how their approaches were similar in degree but different in method and purpose.
Strong answer includes: Both systems represented the most extreme end of the spectrum of state economic control — both essentially abolished meaningful economic freedom for individuals. But their mechanisms and ideological justifications differed sharply. Fascist Italy used "corporatism": the state didn't seize ownership of businesses, but it took control of them by directing what industries, unions, and businesses were allowed to do, subordinating all economic activity to the goals of the nationalist state. Private property still technically existed, but the state told it what to do. The Soviet command economy went further: it abolished private ownership entirely. Stalin's Five-Year Plans placed every factory, farm, and shop under direct state control — there was no market to direct because there was no market. The key difference in purpose: fascism used economic control to serve aggressive nationalism (making Italy great, building military power); Soviet command economics used it to serve communist ideology (eliminating class distinctions through state ownership of all means of production). Both paths led to horrifying human costs — but different kinds.

Reflection question: Despite their enormous differences, what did all three of these systems share? Write 2–3 sentences — this is the video's key insight.
Despite radical ideological differences, all three systems dramatically and permanently expanded the government's role in managing economic life. Before the Depression, the idea that a government was responsible for the health of the economy was considered a radical fringe position — the mainstream view was that markets should be left alone. After the Depression, every major government — democratic, fascist, or communist — accepted as a permanent truth that the state must actively manage economic outcomes. The old world of laissez-faire was gone for good, regardless of whether your government was saving capitalism, redirecting it, or abolishing it.
Activity 2D · Apply · Economic Nationalism Beyond Europe
Prompt: The video notes that the Depression's economic responses weren't just European — Mexico nationalized its oil industry and Brazil's Vargas created the Estado Novo. Explain why the Depression created conditions where even developing nations expanded state economic control, and what they hoped to gain.
Strong answer includes: The Depression collapsed commodity prices globally, devastating economies like Mexico and Brazil that depended heavily on exporting raw materials (oil, coffee, rubber) to wealthy industrial nations. When prices crashed, government revenues collapsed and unemployment soared. At the same time, the Depression exposed how vulnerable these economies were to decisions made in New York, London, or Paris — external shocks they had no power to control. Economic nationalism was a direct response to this vulnerability: by seizing control of key industries (Mexico's nationalization of U.S.-owned oil companies in 1938) or centralizing economic power (Vargas's Estado Novo), governments hoped to insulate their countries from foreign economic domination and capture the profits that had been flowing to foreign investors. The goal was not just economic recovery but economic sovereignty — the right to control your own resources and set your own development agenda rather than serving as a raw materials supplier for wealthier nations.

Activity 3B · SAQ Practice · AP-Style Short Answer
AP Prompt: "Explain how the Great Depression contributed to the expansion of state power in ONE country during the interwar period."

Choose ONE country. Write 2–3 sentences: identify the Depression's impact, explain the government's specific response, and connect it to the expansion of state power.
Sample Response — Soviet Union:

In the Soviet Union, the interwar economic crisis accelerated Stalin's already-radical agenda to abolish market economics entirely and replace it with a state-directed command economy through the Five-Year Plans. Stalin used the global Depression as evidence that capitalism was self-destructing, justifying the forced collectivization of all private farmland — an act that transferred ownership of the most basic means of production (land) from individuals to the state. While the resulting man-made famines killed millions, especially in Ukraine, the Soviet Union did industrialize at breakneck speed, which Stalin presented as proof of communism's superiority — an expansion of state power so complete that not a single factory, farm, or business remained outside government control.

AP note: This response names the country, explains the Depression's role as context, identifies a specific policy (collectivization/Five-Year Plans), and connects it explicitly to the expansion of state power. All four elements are necessary. Notice it doesn't just say "the government grew" — it shows how and why.
Activity 3C · CCOT Analysis · The Government's Role in the Economy
Prompt: What changed and what continued in the relationship between governments and economies from the pre-Depression era (c. 1870–1929) to the post-Depression interwar period (c. 1929–1939)? Give one specific example of each.
What CHANGED
What CONTINUED
CHANGE: The most fundamental change was the permanent end of laissez-faire as the governing economic philosophy of major states. Before 1929, the mainstream position — across most democratic, liberal governments — was that markets were self-correcting and government intervention was counterproductive. After the Depression proved markets could collapse catastrophically without correction, every major government accepted state economic responsibility as necessary. The New Deal's creation of Social Security, the FDIC, and unemployment insurance in the U.S. institutionalized a level of government involvement that would have been considered radical socialism a generation earlier.

CONTINUITY: Despite the radical expansion of state power, the fundamental competition among great powers for economic dominance and resource control continued unchanged. Whether through Keynesian reform, fascist corporatism, or Soviet command planning, states were still competing to maximize their relative power and industrial capacity. Economic nationalism (Germany's rearmament, Japan's resource expansion, the U.S. tariffs) reflected the same logic of competitive great-power rivalry that had driven imperialism — just expressed through economic rather than colonial means.

Activity 4A · Contextualization Builder · AP Essay Skill

Choose one option and write a 3–4 sentence contextualization paragraph connecting a broader historical development to the interwar economic crisis.

Option A: How did the Industrial Revolution create the globally interconnected economic system that made the 1929 crash in the United States into a worldwide depression rather than a local crisis?
Option B: How did the Treaty of Versailles (1919) create the specific economic conditions — German reparations, instability — that made the interwar period so economically fragile?
Option C: How did the rise of socialist and communist ideology in the late 19th century (Marx, Engels) create the intellectual framework that made the Soviet command economy seem like a viable — even appealing — alternative to capitalism during the Depression?
Sample Contextualization — Treaty of Versailles:

The economic chaos of the interwar period cannot be understood without first examining the deliberate economic punishment embedded in the 1919 Treaty of Versailles. The treaty imposed crushing war reparations on Germany — eventually totaling 132 billion gold marks — along with the seizure of German industrial territories, the loss of merchant shipping, and mandatory transfers of livestock and raw materials. These terms weren't just punitive; they were structurally destabilizing. Germany was forced to pay debts far beyond its capacity to generate revenues, leading directly to hyperinflation in the early 1920s when the Weimar government printed money to cover obligations. The reparations system also created dangerous interdependencies: Germany borrowed American money (the Dawes Plan) to pay reparations to France and Britain, who used those payments to repay WWI debts to the United States. This circular system meant that when the 1929 American crash dried up credit, the entire chain collapsed simultaneously — making the global depression far more severe than it would otherwise have been.
Activity 4B · Cross-Unit Synthesis · Connecting Forward
Synthesis Prompt: The video argues that the Depression created a "governance vacuum" — desperate populations lost faith in democratic governments, opening the door for extremists. In 3–5 sentences, connect this argument from 7.4 to a specific development in 7.5 or 7.6 (Tensions After WWI / Causes of WWII). Be explicit about the connection.
Sample Synthesis — Connecting 7.4 to 7.6 (Hitler's Rise):

The governance vacuum the video identifies in 7.4 is the direct mechanism through which the Depression caused WWII. Hitler did not seize power in a coup — he was appointed chancellor legally, as the leader of the largest party in a democratic parliament. That democratic parliament had lost its legitimacy because the Weimar Republic, Germany's post-WWI democratic government, was seen as responsible for both the humiliation of Versailles and the economic devastation of the Depression. German hyperinflation had already destroyed the savings of the middle class in the early 1920s; the 1929 crash then wiped out millions of jobs. When democratic leaders appeared powerless — or worse, complicit with the banks and foreign powers who had imposed these conditions — the political space opened for a party that promised national greatness, economic restoration, and most crucially, someone to blame. The Nazi Party's electoral surge in 1930–1932 was almost perfectly correlated with unemployment figures: as jobs disappeared, Nazi votes multiplied. The Depression didn't just damage the economy — it destroyed the political legitimacy of the system that was supposed to protect people from exactly this kind of catastrophe.
