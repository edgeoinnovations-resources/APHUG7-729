7.6 — Causes of World War II

WWII was not the work of one villain — it was a cascade of governance failures at every level: a punitive peace, a Great Depression governance vacuum, unchecked imperial aggression, and totalitarian states that made war into official state policy.

The Three Levels of Governance Failure — Core Framework

🌐 International Level

The Treaty of Versailles — a punitive peace that humiliated rather than stabilized Germany. The League of Nations proved a paper tiger, powerless to stop Japanese and Italian aggression in the 1930s.

🏛 National Level

Democratic governments destabilized by the Great Depression's "governance vacuum." Economic collapse created an opening for extremist parties promising strength, national greatness, and someone to blame.

⚙️ Institutional Level

Totalitarian regimes (Nazi Germany, Fascist Italy, Imperial Japan) used the full power of the state for persecution, militarism, and conquest — making aggressive war not a last resort but a core state purpose.

Explain Drill 1 — The Governance Vacuum

Explain how the Great Depression created a "governance vacuum" in democratic nations and how that vacuum enabled the rise of totalitarian movements. Use at least one specific example in your answer.
The governance vacuum is the video's key concept for linking economic collapse to political catastrophe. When the Great Depression hit after 1929, democratic governments had no effective toolkit for reversing it. Global production fell 30% in just three years — millions were unemployed, hungry, and watching their life savings evaporate while elected leaders appeared paralyzed. This created not just economic misery but a profound crisis of faith in democratic governance itself: if the system could not protect people in crisis, why remain loyal to it? In Germany, this dynamic was especially explosive. The Weimar Republic had already been fatally undermined by the Treaty of Versailles — the war guilt clause made its leaders look like traitors, and reparations had crushed the economy even before the global crash. The Great Depression delivered the killing blow to its credibility. The Nazi Party surged in democratic elections as desperate voters sought anyone offering certainty, strength, and a clear enemy to blame. Hitler offered all three: national greatness, a coherent ideology, and Jewish people and political opponents as convenient scapegoats. The governance vacuum did not create fascism — the ideological ingredients existed before — but it created the democratic opening through which fascism legally seized state power and then dismantled the democracy that elected it.

Explain Drill 2 — Why Appeasement Failed

Explain why Britain and France adopted the policy of appeasement toward Hitler in the 1930s, and explain why that policy was a "fatal miscalculation." Use the Munich Agreement as your primary specific example.
Appeasement was not naive or cowardly — it was a calculated policy rooted in understandable reasoning. Britain and France were both haunted by WWI's catastrophic human cost and struggling with Depression-era economic crises. Their leaders genuinely believed another total war would be civilizationally devastating, and that Hitler's demands — framed as limited and based on restoring German populations under foreign rule — might be satisfied if met promptly. The logic was: concession over confrontation. The Munich Agreement of 1938 was the policy's clearest and most disastrous expression. Britain and France pressured Czechoslovakia to surrender the Sudetenland to Germany without even inviting Czech representatives to the negotiations. British Prime Minister Neville Chamberlain returned home claiming "peace for our time." Within months, Hitler violated every commitment made at Munich and occupied the remainder of Czechoslovakia. The video's analysis is precise: every concession emboldened Hitler rather than satisfying him. He had learned that the democracies would not fight, that he could break promises without consequences, and that the mere threat of war would always produce more territorial gains than war itself. The fatal miscalculation was applying normal diplomatic logic to a regime whose ideology — Lebensraum, ultranationalism, racial struggle — made aggressive expansionist war not a last resort but the primary purpose and mission of the state.

The League of Nations' failure to impose meaningful consequences on Japan's 1931 invasion of Manchuria was arguably the most enabling act in the cascade. By exposing collective security as a paper tiger before any European crisis materialized, it announced to every aggressive state that the international system would condemn but not stop territorial conquest. Italy watched this and concluded it could take Ethiopia; Hitler watched both and concluded the democracies lacked the will to enforce international norms. Each unpunished act of aggression became a precedent for the next. Britain and France's appeasement at Munich reinforced this at the national level, but the institutional groundwork had been laid by the League's earlier failure. The argument for the League's failure as most significant: it was the hinge between WWI's flawed peace and WWII's unleashed aggression — the moment the world demonstrated that aggression had no real price.

Apply Activity — Governance Weaponized: The Nuremberg Laws

The video describes the Nuremberg Laws as "governance being used as a weapon." Apply this concept to explain how the Nazi state used legal authority — rather than random mob violence — to build the institutional foundation for the Holocaust. Why does the legal mechanism matter historically?
The Nuremberg Laws of 1935 formalized racial hierarchy into German legal code: Jewish people were stripped of citizenship, prohibited from marrying non-Jews, and subjected to a bureaucratic definition of "Jewishness" based on ancestry. The video's insight — that this was governance weaponized — is historically essential for two reasons. First, the legal mechanism gave persecution a veneer of state legitimacy that made it difficult to resist and easy to administer systematically. All institutions of the German state — courts, civil registries, schools, police — became instruments of discrimination. Citizens who enforced the laws were following them, not breaking them. That distinction matters enormously for both perpetrators (it provided moral cover) and victims (it foreclosed legal remedies). Second, the laws served as administrative infrastructure for escalation. Once Jewish people had been legally defined as non-citizens and physically isolated through forced ghetto residence and economic exclusion, they became structurally vulnerable in ways that made subsequent stages — organized violence, deportation, mass murder — bureaucratically feasible. Kristallnacht (1938), staged to appear as a spontaneous riot, was state-organized terror built directly on the legal foundations already laid. For historians, the legal mechanism matters because mass atrocities require institutional scaffolding, not just hatred. Hatred produces pogroms; hatred plus state infrastructure plus totalizing ideology produces the Holocaust. This connects forward to 7.8, where the same three-step pattern (define → wield state power → justify) recurs across multiple 20th-century atrocities.

AP-Style Short Answer Question (SAQ)

Use the following claim to answer parts A, B, and C:

"The rise of fascist and militarist regimes in the 1930s was the direct result of the failure of international and domestic governance structures in the aftermath of World War I."

A. Identify ONE specific way the Treaty of Versailles contributed to political instability in Germany.
B. Explain ONE way the Great Depression enabled the rise of totalitarian movements in Europe.
C. Explain ONE way the failure of the League of Nations emboldened aggressive states in the 1930s.
A. The Treaty of Versailles's war guilt clause (Article 231) forced Germany's new Weimar Republic to officially accept sole responsibility for the war, making its democratic leaders appear as traitors from the moment the republic was founded. Combined with crippling reparation payments that destroyed Germany's economy, this set up Germany's first democratic government to fail — not because democracy itself was weak, but because an international treaty had been specifically designed in ways that made it appear incompetent and illegitimate in the eyes of German voters.

B. The Great Depression created a "governance vacuum" — a crisis of faith in democratic governments that appeared completely powerless to reverse economic collapse. In Germany, unemployment and poverty reached catastrophic levels while the Weimar Republic had no effective response. This desperation created an opening for the Nazi Party, which promised strong leadership, national renewal, and a clear enemy to blame for Germany's suffering. Hitler was legally appointed chancellor in 1933 through democratic elections precisely because millions of voters had lost faith in the existing system. The Depression didn't create fascism, but it gave fascism the democratic opening it needed to take power legally.

C. When Japan invaded Manchuria in 1931 and established the puppet state Manchukuo, the League of Nations issued condemnations but imposed no meaningful military or economic consequences. Japan simply withdrew from the League and continued its expansion. This episode proved to every aggressive state that the League's collective security system was a paper tiger. Italy repeated the same gamble in Ethiopia (1935–36) with the same result. By the time Hitler began annexing territory in the late 1930s, the pattern was clear: aggression would be condemned but not stopped. The League's institutional failure to act taught aggressors that there were no real consequences for territorial expansion.

CCOT Analysis — International Order c. 1919–1939

🔄 What Changed
➡️ What Continued
What Changed: The post-WWI era introduced genuinely novel international governance frameworks. The League of Nations was the first sustained attempt at collective security. The Kellogg-Briand Pact (1928) attempted to outlaw war as an instrument of national policy. The ideological landscape also shifted dramatically: fascism and Soviet communism represented new political alternatives to liberal democracy that had not existed before WWI, creating a three-way ideological competition shaping every foreign policy decision. Nazi Germany's use of mass media, spectacle, and terror to build political loyalty also represented a new level of state control over public opinion.

What Continued: Despite new institutional frameworks, the underlying dynamics of great-power competition, imperial ambition, and nationalist ideology continued uninterrupted. Major powers still pursued territorial expansion when they calculated they could get away with it. Economic competition still drove imperial rivalry — Japan's invasion of Manchuria was explicitly driven by resource needs during the Depression. Nationalist ideology continued to destabilize multi-ethnic states and fuel expansionist demands, just as it had before 1914. Most importantly, the fundamental anarchic structure of the international system continued: it had no enforcement mechanism beyond the collective willingness of powerful states to act, and when those states lacked that will — due to war-weariness, economic crisis, or domestic political pressures — the system was functionally powerless.

Option B — The Rise of Ultranationalism (c. 1880s–1920s)

Option C — U.S. Rejection of the League of Nations (1919–1920)

Explain how the U.S. Senate's rejection of the League of Nations fatally weakened the only institution designed to prevent another great-power war — and how that founding weakness set up the League's catastrophic failures in the 1930s.

World War I was the first industrial-scale total war in human history, and its psychological legacy fundamentally shaped the political decisions of the 1930s. Between 1914 and 1918, roughly 20 million people died in a conflict that destroyed four empires, bankrupted entire nations, and shattered the 19th-century belief in European civilization as a force for progress. By the interwar period, an entire generation of political leaders had witnessed this catastrophe firsthand; for British and French leaders particularly, the memory of WWI made the prospect of another major war seem so unthinkable that almost any alternative appeared preferable. This war-weariness was the psychological engine of appeasement: when Hitler remilitarized the Rhineland in 1936 or annexed Austria in 1938, British and French leaders were making a calculated bet that concession was better than returning to the trenches. The tragedy was that this rational fear of repeating WWI made repeating it more likely, not less — by signaling that the democracies would not fight, appeasement removed the one deterrent that might have restrained Hitler's expansionism. The psychological legacy of WWI is therefore essential context for understanding why the cascade of governance failures identified in 7.6 was so difficult to interrupt even as it visibly accelerated through the 1930s.

Cross-Unit Synthesis — 7.6 → 7.8 (Mass Atrocities After 1900)

The video explains how Nazi Germany used the Nuremberg Laws to strip Jewish people of citizenship and Kristallnacht to escalate from legal persecution to state-engineered violence. Connect this progression to 7.8's framework for understanding mass atrocities. How does understanding 7.6's governance failures help explain how the Holocaust became possible — not just as an act of hatred, but as a product of specific institutional choices?
The connection between 7.6's governance failure argument and 7.8's mass atrocity framework is direct and essential: the Holocaust did not happen despite the Nazi state — it happened through it, using the full institutional power of a modern bureaucratic government. Section 7.8 describes a three-step pattern through which mass atrocities unfold: (1) define the "other" through propaganda and law, (2) wield state power — turning the police, courts, and bureaucracy into weapons against the targeted group, and (3) justify the violence with an ideology that makes extermination seem necessary. Section 7.6 provides the political history of how a state came to exist that was willing and able to execute all three steps. The Treaty of Versailles and the Great Depression created the governance vacuum in which the Nazi Party rose to legal power. Once in power, Hitler systematically dismantled Weimar democratic institutions and replaced them with a totalitarian state whose authority derived entirely from the Führer's will. The Nuremberg Laws were step one of the 7.8 pattern at scale: defining Jewish people as non-citizens built the legal and social infrastructure for what followed. Kristallnacht was the hinge between steps one and two — moving from legal exclusion to organized physical terror behind a façade of spontaneity. The Holocaust was the completion of steps two and three: state power fully weaponized against a legally defined and socially isolated target population, justified by racial ideology. Without 7.6's governance failure story — how a democratic state legally transformed itself into a totalitarian one — the Holocaust appears to arise from nowhere. With it, the atrocity becomes historically explicable as the product of traceable institutional decisions made across more than a decade.
