How the very systems built to prevent war became the gears of a machine that drove the world into catastrophe

Reference · The MAIN Causes — Overview
⚔ Militarism

Governments poured money into massive arms races — Britain and Germany especially competed to build bigger navies and armies. Military buildup became a way to project power and signal readiness for war.

🤝 Alliances

Secret mutual-defense treaties created two armed camps: Allied Powers (France, Britain, Russia) vs. Central Powers (Germany, Austria-Hungary). A conflict between any two nations could drag in all the others automatically.

🌍 Imperialism

The "scramble" for colonies in Africa and Asia created intense rivalry and friction between European powers. Control of colonies meant access to raw materials, markets, and global prestige — all worth fighting over.

🏴 Nationalism

A double-edged sword: governments used nationalism to rally public support for military buildup, but in multi-ethnic empires (Austria-Hungary), ethnic nationalist movements threatened to tear the state apart from within.

Activity 1C · Fact Recall · Choose the best answer
1. According to the video, what was the fundamental flaw in the alliance system?
✓ Correct. The video describes alliances as a "tripwire" — a system designed to prevent war by creating mutual deterrence, but which actually guaranteed that any local conflict would automatically escalate into a continent-wide war. Individual governments lost the ability to choose de-escalation.
2. How did imperialism directly contribute to the outbreak of WWI?
✓ Correct. The scramble for colonies created "massive friction and rivalry" between European governments who were competing for the same territories, resources, and global prestige. This heightened mutual suspicion and made diplomatic cooperation much harder by 1914.
3. Why did the video describe nationalism as a "double-edged sword" for European governments?
✓ Correct. Germany used nationalism to rally public support for its military buildup and imperial ambitions. But Austria-Hungary — which ruled over dozens of ethnic groups (Serbs, Croats, Czechs, and more) — found that nationalism was a direct existential threat, as each group wanted its own independent nation.
4. Who was Gavrilo Princip, and what was the significance of his act?
✓ Correct. The video is explicit that the assassination was "not the deep cause" but "the spark that lit the fuse of a system that was just waiting to explode." Princip was a Serbian nationalist whose goal was to end Austro-Hungarian rule in the Balkans — which is why Austria-Hungary's response was so extreme.
5. How did the war's scope become truly "global"?
✓ Correct. The video emphasizes this point with a striking image: Indian soldiers fighting for Britain in a war that had nothing to do with them. Because European empires had colonized much of the globe, when those empires went to war, they "dragged the rest of the world in with them." Over 1.5 million soldiers from India alone fought for Britain.

Activity 2A · Explain Drill · Systemic Failure
Prompt: The video's central argument is that WWI was a "complete failure of the systems of international governance." Explain what this means using two specific MAIN causes as examples.
Strong answer includes: The phrase "systemic failure" means that the problem wasn't any one event or villain — it was that the structures designed to manage international relations all broke down simultaneously. The alliance system is the clearest example: it was built to prevent war through deterrence, but its fatal design meant that once any two nations started fighting, all others were automatically pulled in. Diplomacy — individual governments choosing peace — was effectively removed from the equation. Militarism reinforced this failure: governments had spent decades building up massive armies and navies, which meant they had enormous destructive capacity ready to deploy and military leaders who had been planning for war. When the crisis hit, the military machine was already running.
Activity 2B · Explain Drill · Nationalism as Double-Edged Sword
Prompt: Explain why nationalism was useful for some governments but existentially threatening to others. Use Germany and Austria-Hungary as contrasting examples.
Strong answer includes: For Germany, nationalism was a powerful tool of governance — the government used it to build public enthusiasm for military expansion, colonial acquisitions, and the idea that Germany deserved a place among the great powers. Citizens were told that national strength was German strength. But Austria-Hungary faced the opposite problem: it was a multi-ethnic empire ruling over Serbs, Croats, Czechs, Poles, and dozens of other groups, none of whom considered themselves "Austro-Hungarian." For these peoples, nationalism meant the right to self-determination — to break away from Austria-Hungary and form their own nations. The assassination of Franz Ferdinand was a direct product of Serbian nationalism in action: the assassin Gavrilo Princip wanted to liberate Slavic peoples from Austrian rule. So nationalism simultaneously gave Germany a reason to build a war machine AND gave Austria-Hungary a reason to fear the Balkans as a permanent threat to its existence.

Reflection question: What structural advantage did the Allied Powers have over the Central Powers, and why did this matter in a long war?
The Allied Powers' most significant structural advantage was their colonial reach. Britain and France could draw on soldiers, supplies, and resources from massive global empires — over 1.5 million Indian soldiers alone fought for Britain. This meant the Allies could replenish human losses in a way Germany and Austria-Hungary could not. Germany faced the classic two-front war problem (fighting France in the west and Russia in the east simultaneously), but crucially, it had no equivalent global reserve of manpower to draw on. In a prolonged industrial war of attrition, this structural imbalance eventually proved decisive.

Activity 3B · SAQ Practice · AP-Style Short Answer
AP Prompt: "Explain how ONE of the following contributed to the outbreak of World War I: militarism, alliances, imperialism, or nationalism."

Choose ONE cause. Write 2–3 sentences: name the cause, explain its role, and provide at least one specific piece of evidence.
Sample Response — Alliances:

The alliance system contributed to the outbreak of WWI by transforming a regional dispute into an automatic continent-wide war. When Austria-Hungary declared war on Serbia on July 28, 1914, the system's mutual-defense obligations immediately activated: Russia mobilized to defend Serbia, Germany declared war on Russia to defend Austria-Hungary, and France and Britain entered because of their obligations to Russia — all within five weeks of the original assassination. The fatal flaw was that the system removed each government's ability to de-escalate individually; once the first domino fell, no single leader could stop the chain reaction. The video's argument is that this "tripwire" design turned a governance tool meant to prevent war into the primary mechanism of escalation.

AP Skill note: This response names the cause, explains its mechanism (how it worked), gives specific chronological evidence (the 37-day timeline), and connects to the broader argument (systemic failure). All four elements strengthen a SAQ response.
Activity 3C · CCOT Analysis · Continuity and Change in International Relations
Prompt: What continued and what changed in how nations managed international relations from the Congress of Vienna (1815) to the outbreak of WWI (1914)? Give one specific example of each.
What CHANGED
What CONTINUED
CHANGE: The most significant change from the Congress of Vienna era to 1914 was the shift from flexible concert diplomacy to rigid automatic alliances. The Concert of Europe (post-1815) was designed to allow great powers to negotiate crises case-by-case through multilateral conferences — and it largely worked for nearly a century. By 1914, this flexibility had been replaced by secret bilateral alliances that locked nations into automatic obligations, making crisis management nearly impossible. The 1839 Treaty of London guaranteeing Belgian neutrality — which Britain used to enter the war — is a perfect example of how old diplomatic commitments now activated automatically.

CONTINUITY: What continued was the fundamental assumption that great power rivalry and competition were normal features of international relations. The Congress of Vienna didn't end rivalry — it tried to manage it. By 1914, European powers were still competing for colonies, military dominance, and prestige, just as they had in the 18th and 19th centuries. The actors changed but the underlying logic of power competition persisted.

Activity 4A · Contextualization Builder · AP Essay Skill

Choose one of the three prompts and write a 3–4 sentence contextualization paragraph that connects a broader historical development to the causes of WWI.

Option A: How did the Industrial Revolution (c. 1750–1900) create the military technologies and economic systems that made WWI so catastrophic?
Option B: How did the "New Imperialism" of the late 19th century (the scramble for Africa and Asia) create the rivalries and tensions that made European cooperation impossible by 1914?
Option C: How did the 19th-century spread of nationalist ideologies (from the French Revolution and Napoleonic Wars) plant the seeds that destabilized empires by 1914?
Sample Contextualization — Industrial Revolution:

The Industrial Revolution fundamentally transformed the nature of warfare in the century before WWI. New manufacturing technologies allowed governments to produce weapons at industrial scale — rifles, artillery, battleships, and eventually machine guns and poison gas — at costs and speeds unimaginable in the pre-industrial era. The same economic logic that drove factory production drove military production: efficiency, scale, and continuous innovation. By 1914, European governments had spent decades in an arms race fueled by industrial capacity, building armies and navies that were not only much larger than before but qualitatively more destructive. This industrialization of military power meant that when the alliance system finally triggered a conflict, the war that followed was not a limited cabinet war of the 18th century but a total industrialized catastrophe that consumed entire economies and populations. The scale of WWI's destruction — ultimately 20 million dead — was only possible because industrial technology had made killing efficient.
Activity 4B · Cross-Unit Synthesis · Connecting Forward
Synthesis Prompt: The video argues that "the entire 20th century was shaped by this one breakdown." In 3–5 sentences, connect the causes or consequences of WWI (from 7.2) to a specific development in a later section of Unit 7 (7.3–7.9). Be explicit about the connection.
Sample Synthesis — Connecting 7.2 to 7.6 (Causes of WWII):

The systemic failure that produced WWI directly created the conditions for WWII twenty years later. The alliance system's collapse left four empires destroyed and a humiliated Germany forced to sign the Treaty of Versailles, which the video describes as a "fragile, really flawed peace." This peace imposed crushing reparations and war guilt on Germany, generating the economic desperation and national resentment that Hitler exploited. The same governance failure pattern repeated: international institutions (the League of Nations) were designed to prevent conflict but lacked enforcement power, just as pre-WWI alliances lacked conflict-resolution mechanisms. When Japan invaded Manchuria and Italy attacked Ethiopia in the 1930s, the League's inability to respond sent the same message that pre-1914 diplomacy had sent — that aggressive nations would face no real consequences. In this sense, 7.2's central lesson — that governance systems designed to prevent war can actually cause it — applies just as directly to the causes of WWII.
