Activity 2A · Describe Drill · Internal Weaknesses
Prompt: Describe how internal weaknesses made land-based empires vulnerable to collapse after 1900. Your answer should identify at least two specific examples from two different empires.
Strong answer includes: Land-based empires faced overlapping internal crises that made reform nearly impossible. In Qing China, ethnic tension between the ruling Manchu minority and the Han Chinese majority created constant instability — the dynasty ruled a people who saw it as foreign. In the Ottoman Empire, a failing agrarian economy left the government without tax revenue to modernize its military or infrastructure. In Russia, Tsar Nicholas II refused to share political power even as industrialization created a new urban workforce demanding representation. These internal "cracks" meant that any external shock — like a military defeat or a world war — could bring the entire structure down. As the video notes, they were "sitting ducks" — too rigid to adapt.
Activity 2B · Explain Drill · Power Vacuum
Prompt: Explain why the collapse of empires after 1900 did not lead to peace. What rushed in to fill the power vacuum, and why did this cause conflict?
Strong answer includes: When empires collapse, they do not simply disappear — they leave behind a dangerous void where no clear authority exists. After 1900, this "power vacuum" was filled not by one unified vision but by competing and incompatible ideologies: communism (Bolsheviks in Russia), republicanism (Sun Yat-sen in China), and aggressive nationalism (Young Turks, later fascism). These systems all claimed the right to organize society differently, making compromise extremely difficult. In Russia, the Bolsheviks didn't just want new leadership — they wanted to re-engineer society from its foundations. In the Ottoman Empire, Turkification tried to impose one identity on a diverse population. Each ideology was certain it was right, turning political disagreements into violent struggles that would define the rest of the 20th century.

Reflection question: What is the most significant similarity between these two revolutions? What is the most significant difference? Write 2–3 sentences.
Most significant similarity: Both revolutions were triggered by the failure of an ancient dynastic system to adapt to modern pressures — the Romanovs and the Qing both collapsed under the weight of internal contradictions and external shocks.

Most significant difference: The ideologies that replaced those dynasties were fundamentally opposed — Lenin's communism sought to abolish private property and create a classless society run by the state, while Sun Yat-sen's vision preserved individual rights within a democratic republic. This difference would define Cold War conflict 30 years later.

Activity 3B · SAQ Practice · AP-Style Short Answer
AP Prompt: "Explain how the collapse of ONE land-based empire after 1900 contributed to instability in the early twentieth century."

Write 2–3 sentences. Name the empire, identify a specific cause of collapse, and connect it to a form of instability that followed.
Sample Response: The Russian Empire collapsed in 1917 after a cascade of internal and external pressures, including the humiliating defeat in the Russo-Japanese War, the Bloody Sunday massacre of 1905, and the catastrophic cost of World War I, which left troops under-armed and civilians starving. This collapse contributed to instability because it created a power vacuum that the Bolsheviks filled with a radical communist ideology — not just a new government, but a complete re-engineering of society that abolished private property and concentrated all power in the state. The establishment of a communist state in Russia subsequently inspired revolutionary movements globally and set up the ideological conflict between communism and capitalism that defined the rest of the twentieth century.

AP Skill at work: Causation — this response identifies both a cause of collapse (WWI crisis) AND traces the effect to a broader development (global ideological conflict). Both are needed for full credit.
Activity 3C · CCOT Analysis · Continuity and Change Over Time
Prompt: What continued and what changed in the nature of political governance from 1900 to the 1920s? Give one specific example of each.
What CHANGED in governance
What CONTINUED in governance
CHANGE: The most dramatic change was the collapse of dynastic monarchy as the dominant form of government. By the 1920s, the Russian Romanov, Chinese Qing, and Ottoman dynasties — all of which had ruled for centuries — had been swept away and replaced by entirely new political systems (communism in Russia, a republic in China, and a nationalist Turkish state). The idea that a single emperor held absolute, divinely-sanctioned power was effectively destroyed in this period.

CONTINUITY: Despite the rhetoric of liberation, the concentration of political power in the hands of a small elite continued. In Russia, the Tsar's autocracy was replaced not by democracy but by Bolshevik one-party rule. In Mexico, the PRI created a "stable but not always democratic" government that would control politics for 70 years. The faces changed; the concentration of power often did not.

AP Tip: The best CCOT answers avoid oversimplified "everything changed" or "nothing changed" responses. The most sophisticated answers find the change within the continuity — in this case, the form of authoritarian governance changed, but its existence did not.

Activity 4A · Contextualization Builder · AP Essay Skill

Contextualization means connecting your essay topic to a broader historical development that preceded or surrounded it. Choose one of the prompts below and write a 3–4 sentence contextualization paragraph.

Option A: How did 19th-century European imperialism create the conditions that made these empires vulnerable by 1900?
Option B: How did the Industrial Revolution create new pressures that traditional land-based empires were structurally unable to handle?
Option C: How did Enlightenment ideas about self-determination and popular sovereignty create expectations that imperial subjects would eventually demand?
Sample Contextualization — Enlightenment & Self-Determination:

The Enlightenment of the 17th and 18th centuries fundamentally reshaped how people thought about political authority. Thinkers like John Locke and Jean-Jacques Rousseau argued that legitimate government derived not from God or hereditary right, but from the consent of the governed. These ideas spread through the Atlantic revolutions of the late 18th century — the American (1776) and French (1789) revolutions demonstrated that ordinary people could overthrow entrenched dynasties and build new systems based on popular sovereignty. By 1900, these ideals had circulated through educated classes in Russia, China, and the Ottoman Empire, creating populations who now had a language of rights to describe their dissatisfaction with autocratic rule. This made the internal crises of land-based empires especially combustible — the people suffering under these systems increasingly believed not just that things were bad, but that they had a right to demand change.

AP Tip: A strong contextualization paragraph does three things: (1) describes the broader context, (2) explains how it relates to your essay topic, and (3) makes the connection specific — not just "the Enlightenment happened," but "the Enlightenment created the ideological conditions that made revolution seem legitimate."
Activity 4B · Cross-Unit Synthesis · Connecting Forward
Synthesis Prompt: The video ends with this claim: "The collapse of empires didn't bring peace — it unleashed a fierce battle of ideologies that defined the rest of the 20th century."

In 3–5 sentences, explain how the developments of 7.1 (empire collapse, power vacuums, competing ideologies) help explain at least one event or development from a later period in this unit (7.2–7.9).
Sample Synthesis — Connecting 7.1 to 7.6 (Causes of WWII):

The power vacuum created by WWI and the collapse of the Ottoman and Austro-Hungarian empires directly shaped the conditions that produced World War II. The Treaty of Versailles (1919) attempted to fill these vacuums by carving new nation-states from former imperial territories — but these borders often ignored ethnic and cultural realities, creating resentful, unstable states. In Germany, the war guilt clause and reparations humiliated a proud nation, providing fertile ground for an extreme nationalist ideology (fascism) to promise restoration of greatness. Mussolini in Italy and Hitler in Germany essentially exploited the same power-vacuum logic described in 7.1 — promising to fill the void of weak democratic governments with strong, ideologically unified states. The "fierce battle of ideologies" that began with competing visions of communism, republicanism, and nationalism after 1900 escalated into the fascism vs. democracy vs. communism triangle that made World War II inevitable.
