📽 The Central Argument — Read Before You Begin

The video's driving question is simple: Who gets to rule? After WWI, the promise of self-determination was made loudly and broken quietly. Imperial powers used the mandate system — colonialism dressed up as international law — to keep controlling millions of people. Japan did the same through puppet states like Manchukuo. The colonized world refused to accept this betrayal. From Gandhi's Satyagraha in India to labor strikes in West Africa, a global wave of resistance proved that the systems of governance set up after WWI were ultimately a failure. Use these materials to trace that arc: broken promise → masked empire → fierce resistance → unresolved contradiction. That contradiction set the stage for decolonization after WWII.

Explain Drill 1 — The Mandate System

Explain how the mandate system functioned as a continuation of imperialism rather than a step toward self-determination. Use specific territorial examples in your answer.
The mandate system, created at the Paris Peace Conference of 1919 under the League of Nations, was framed as a temporary guardianship to prepare "less advanced" peoples for eventual self-rule. However, in practice it was colonialism with a fancy legalistic makeover. Rather than freeing the old German and Ottoman territories, the League simply handed them to the victorious European powers. Germany's colony of Cameroon was split between France and Britain. The vast Ottoman lands were divided so that Britain controlled Iraq and Palestine while France took Syria. None of these territories had any meaningful input into the arrangement — and the concept of "tutelage" assumed that European governance was inherently superior, the same racist logic that had justified imperial conquest for centuries. The system did not weaken empire; it expanded it, giving European powers new territories under the respectable cover of international law. For the millions of people living in these mandates, the change in name from "colony" to "mandate" changed nothing about who was in charge or who was benefiting.

Explain Drill 2 — Gandhi's Satyagraha

Explain how Gandhi's Satyagraha was both a political strategy and a profound philosophical challenge to the legitimacy of British rule in India.
Satyagraha — often translated as "truth-force" or "civil disobedience" — was Gandhi's carefully designed strategy for challenging British authority without resorting to violence. Its political logic was brilliant: by peacefully and very publicly breaking laws they considered unjust, Indian participants were making a statement that those laws had no moral authority. The message was direct: we do not recognize your right to govern us. The 1930 Salt March illustrates this perfectly. The British held a monopoly on salt production — it was literally illegal for Indians to collect their own. Gandhi walked 240 miles to the sea and simply picked up a handful of salt from the ground. On the surface it was a minor act of lawbreaking. But it dramatized the absurdity of British economic control in a way that no speech could, and it did so in front of international media. The image of ordinary people being arrested for picking up salt discredited British rule globally. Beyond its tactical genius, Satyagraha was a philosophical challenge: Gandhi was arguing that just governance requires the consent of the governed, and that consent had been permanently withdrawn. The Amritsar Massacre — where British troops had opened fire on an unarmed crowd — had already proven to Indian nationalists that compromise was impossible. Satyagraha mobilized millions of ordinary Indians while keeping the moral high ground.

Reflection: What do all of these cases share, despite their different names and justifications? What does this pattern tell us about the nature of empire in the interwar period?
Across every case — British mandates, Japanese puppet states, French West Africa — the common thread is the gap between the stated justification and the lived reality for colonized peoples. The names change (mandate, co-prosperity sphere, tutelage) but the structure does not: an outside power controls land, labor, and resources while denying the local population political agency. What this pattern reveals is that interwar imperialism was not a collection of isolated policies but a systemic global order. Even Japan, positioning itself as an alternative to Western domination, reproduced the same logic of racial hierarchy and resource extraction. This uniformity across culturally and geographically distinct empires suggests that imperial expansion after WWI was driven by structural incentives — access to resources, strategic territory, and cheap labor — that no ideology could disguise for long. For colonized peoples who had fought in WWI partly on the promise of greater rights, this pattern was not just hypocritical; it was a politicizing betrayal that radicalized independence movements across Africa, Asia, and the Middle East.

Apply Activity — West African Labor Strikes

The video describes West African workers organizing large strikes against French colonial authorities. Apply the concept of "resistance as political challenge" — not just an economic one — to explain the significance of these strikes beyond wage disputes.
When West African workers went on strike against French colonial authorities, they were doing more than demanding better wages — they were challenging the entire political and economic structure of French rule. Colonial economies were built on the extraction of labor at minimal cost; to withhold that labor was to disrupt the very foundation of the colonial enterprise. The strikes therefore functioned as a direct assertion that colonized workers had power, agency, and the right to refuse participation in a system they had not chosen. Like Gandhi's Salt March, these strikes made an implicit political argument: your authority over us depends on our cooperation, and we are withdrawing it. The fact that workers organized collectively across different communities also demonstrated the emergence of a shared anti-colonial identity — an "us" versus "them" consciousness that would be essential for later independence movements. The connection to 7.9 and beyond is significant: labor movements in Africa during the interwar period were early training grounds for the political leaders who would drive decolonization after World War II, proving that economic resistance and political resistance were never truly separate.

AP-Style Short Answer Question (SAQ)

Use the following claim to answer parts A, B, and C:

"The post-WWI international order, rather than resolving the tensions of empire, intensified resistance to colonial rule across Asia and Africa."

A. Identify ONE specific example that supports this claim.
B. Explain ONE way the mandate system functioned as a continuation of imperialism rather than a step toward self-determination.
C. Explain ONE way resistance movements in the interwar period set the stage for decolonization after World War II.
A. One example that supports this claim is the Indian independence movement's radicalization following the Amritsar Massacre of 1919, where British troops killed hundreds of unarmed Indian civilians. This event destroyed any remaining hope for a gradual, negotiated path to independence and pushed the Indian National Congress and leaders like Gandhi toward organized mass resistance through Satyagraha — civil disobedience — rather than petitioning the colonial government.

B. The mandate system continued imperialism by disguising territorial expansion as international guardianship. Under the League of Nations, Britain and France received control over former German and Ottoman territories — including Iraq, Palestine, Syria, and Cameroon — with the stated justification that these peoples needed "tutelage" before they could govern themselves. In practice, the mandate powers administered these territories exactly as colonies: extracting resources, controlling political institutions, and denying local populations meaningful self-governance. The structure was colonialism with a legalistic makeover.

C. Interwar resistance movements built the organizational infrastructure and ideological frameworks that powered post-WWII decolonization. Gandhi's Salt March and Satyagraha demonstrated that mass civil disobedience could delegitimize colonial authority in the eyes of the international community. West African labor strikes showed that colonial economies were vulnerable when workers organized collectively. These movements created experienced political leaders, established networks of nationalist organizations, and developed arguments for sovereignty that would be made again — with greater force — after WWII weakened European imperial states and the UN's founding documents again invoked self-determination.

CCOT Analysis — Imperialism c. 1870–1939

🔄 What Changed
➡️ What Continued
What Changed: After WWI, the overt language of conquest was largely replaced by legalistic frameworks like the mandate system, which framed imperial control as temporary guardianship under international supervision. The League of Nations gave a veneer of multilateral legitimacy to what was effectively bilateral territorial expansion. Japan also introduced a new model — the puppet state — that installed local figureheads (like the last Chinese emperor in Manchukuo) while maintaining external control, offering a non-Western competitor to European imperial methods. Additionally, the scale of organized resistance grew dramatically: from scattered uprisings before WWI to coordinated mass movements led by figures like Gandhi and African labor organizers, using modern communications and nationalist ideology.

What Continued: Despite the new vocabulary, the fundamental structure of empire remained unchanged: European (and Japanese) powers extracted resources, controlled political institutions, maintained racial hierarchies, and denied self-governance to colonized peoples. The economic relationship — colonies as sources of raw materials and cheap labor for industrial metropoles — continued unaltered. The justifying ideology of civilizational hierarchy ("tutelage," "co-prosperity") was new branding for old Social Darwinism. And the core enforcement mechanism — violence against resisters, as demonstrated by Amritsar — remained available and used.

Option C — The role of WWI colonial soldiers

Explain how the mobilization of over a million colonial soldiers from India, Africa, and Asia during WWI — many fighting with explicit or implicit promises of greater rights — created a specific post-war political dynamic in which the continuation of imperial control was experienced as a personal and collective betrayal.

During World War I, the major European empires — particularly Britain and France — mobilized enormous numbers of soldiers from their colonial territories. India alone contributed approximately 1.3 million troops to the British war effort, while hundreds of thousands more came from West Africa, North Africa, and Southeast Asia. Many of these soldiers fought with an understanding, sometimes explicit and sometimes implied, that their military service would be rewarded with greater political rights or steps toward self-governance. This context makes the post-war settlement directly relevant to understanding why resistance movements intensified after 1919: it was not simply that imperial rule continued, but that it continued after a specific, widely understood promise had been made and then broken. The Indian National Congress's explosive growth after the war, the radicalization produced by the Amritsar Massacre, and the West African strikes of the 1920s–1930s all gain additional force when understood against this backdrop of betrayal. Colonial soldiers who had survived industrial warfare returned home not grateful for the peace but politicized by the injustice of it.

Cross-Unit Synthesis — 7.5 → 8.2 (Cold War & Decolonization)

The video argues that the unresolved tensions from WWI "set the stage for the enormous wave of decolonization that would sweep across the globe after World War II." Connect the specific mechanisms of resistance developed in 7.5 to the decolonization movements of Unit 8. How did the interwar period's anti-colonial organizing make post-WWII independence possible?
The anti-colonial movements of the interwar period (7.5) and the decolonization wave of the post-WWII era (Unit 8) are not two separate stories — the second is the direct continuation of the first, supercharged by WWII's destruction of European imperial prestige. In 7.5, the key development was the construction of organized resistance infrastructure: the Indian National Congress became a mass political party under Gandhi's leadership; West African labor movements developed networks of solidarity across colonial borders; nationalist intellectuals across Asia and Africa built ideological frameworks for sovereignty. These were not just protests — they were political movements training for eventual state-building. By 1947, when Britain granted Indian independence (Unit 8), the INC was not a new institution scrambling for power but a seasoned organization that had been organized and disciplined by decades of Satyagraha. Similarly, the leaders of West African independence movements in the 1950s and 1960s had often begun their political careers in the labor organizations of the 1920s and 1930s. The Cold War context (Unit 8) added urgency — both the US and USSR had rhetorical commitments to self-determination that made defending European empire diplomatically costly — but the organizational and ideological groundwork had been laid in the interwar period. The video's central argument holds: the mandate system's failure to resolve the question of "who gets to rule" guaranteed that the question would be answered by force, from below, after the second global war finished what the first began.
