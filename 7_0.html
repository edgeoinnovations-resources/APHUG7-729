MAIN

The four underlying causes of World War I: Militarism, Alliances, Imperialism, Nationalism. Together these forces created a powder keg where a single spark could ignite a global war.
Total War
Click to reveal →
A conflict in which entire societies — factories, civilians, and economies — are mobilized for the war effort. The line between soldier and civilian disappears. WWI and WWII were both fought as total wars.
Causation
Click to reveal →
The AP historical thinking skill of explaining why events happened and what resulted from them — tracing cause-and-effect chains across time. The video's "domino effect" timeline is causation in action.
Continuity & Change (CCOT)
Click to reveal →
The AP skill of tracking what stayed the same and what changed across a time period — and explaining why. The video contrasts the continuity of imperial rivalries with the dramatic change of new ideologies like communism.
Treaty of Versailles
Click to reveal →
The 1919 peace settlement ending WWI. Rather than solving underlying tensions, it punished Germany harshly (war guilt, reparations, territorial losses), planting the seeds for the instability that led to WWII.
Mass Atrocities
Click to reveal →
Deliberate, state-sponsored policies of extermination targeting specific populations. Examples include the Armenian Genocide (WWI), the Holocaust (WWII), Stalin's purges, and the Rwandan Genocide. These are not accidents of war — they are intentional acts.
Fascism
Click to reveal →
An ultranationalist authoritarian ideology that emerged in the interwar period. Fascist regimes (Nazi Germany, Mussolini's Italy) glorified state power, racial superiority, and military expansion. They rose by exploiting the instability created by WWI and the Great Depression.
Interwar Period
Click to reveal →
The years between WWI and WWII (1919–1939). Characterized by unresolved Versailles tensions, the Great Depression, the rise of fascism and communism, and the failure of international institutions to prevent another war.
Historical Argumentation
Click to reveal →
The AP skill of building a sophisticated argument that weighs multiple causes, acknowledges complexity, and argues for the relative significance of different factors. This is what separates a 3 from a 5 on the AP exam.
Contextualization
Click to reveal →
Describing a broader historical context (before, during, or after the period) and connecting it to the topic. On the AP exam, this earns its own point on LEQ and DBQ — and requires 3–5 sentences, not just a phrase.

Stage 3 — Apply: Describe, Explain, Compare

Now use AP command verbs to process the content. These are the same operations tested on SAQs. Write your answers, then expand the sample response to compare.

📌 AP Verb Reminder Describe = relevant characteristics — more than just naming a term.
Explain = show how or why — connect cause to effect. Don't just state what happened.
3A · Describe Drill
Describe How did the four MAIN factors interact to make a major European war almost inevitable once a single incident occurred?
Write 2–3 sentences. Don't just list the four terms — describe how they were connected to each other.
✓ Sample Answer
The rivalry for overseas empires (imperialism) fueled a dangerous arms race (militarism), as European powers poured resources into building massive armies and navies to defend and expand their colonial holdings. Intense nationalism made compromise nearly impossible, as governments faced enormous public pressure to protect national honor at all costs. The interconnected alliance system meant that once a single conflict began, treaty obligations automatically drew every major power in — so the assassination of one archduke in 1914 became the spark that ignited an entire continent.
What earns this: It goes beyond listing — it explains the connections between factors (how one drove another) and shows the chain reaction effect. That's what "describe" requires.
3B · Explain Drill
Explain Why did the Treaty of Versailles fail to produce lasting peace after World War I?
Write 2–3 sentences. Include a cause-and-effect connection — not just "the terms were harsh."
✓ Sample Answer
The Treaty of Versailles failed to produce lasting peace because it punished Germany so severely — through the war guilt clause, massive reparations, and territorial losses — that it created both economic ruin and deep nationalist resentment. This destabilized Germany's fragile new democratic government, making it appear weak and incompetent to its own citizens. When the global economy then collapsed in 1929, those unresolved tensions erupted, allowing fascist movements like the Nazi Party to rise to power by exploiting public anger and promising to reverse the humiliations of Versailles.
What earns this: Shows the mechanism — not just what happened, but why one thing led to the next. This causal chain is exactly what AP graders look for in an "explain" response.
3C · Historical Comparison
Compare The video notes that Western democracies and the Soviet Union responded to the same crisis (the Great Depression) in very different ways. Complete the comparison table, then write one sentence explaining why the difference existed.

Explain In one sentence: why did these two systems choose such different responses?
✓ Sample Answers
Western Democracies — Economic: Largely paralyzed initially; market economies collapsed and governments were slow to intervene. Some (like the US) eventually adopted state programs (New Deal) but recovery was slow and incomplete by the late 1930s.

Soviet Union — Economic: Rapid state-controlled industrialization through Five-Year Plans — forced collectivization of agriculture, crash industrialization of heavy industry, no tolerance for market forces.

Western Democracies — Political: Democratic institutions survived in Britain and the US but were severely strained; in some countries (Germany, Italy) economic desperation opened the door for fascist movements.

Soviet Union — Political: Stalin consolidated totalitarian control; the purges (1936–38) eliminated rivals and millions of perceived enemies, making Soviet power more absolute but at enormous human cost.

Why the difference: The core reason was ideological — capitalist market economies relied on private decisions and profit signals, while the Soviet command economy allowed the state to simply direct resources by decree, making rapid centralized action possible regardless of human cost.
AP Comparison Tip: A real comparison always explains the reason for the difference — here, the answer is fundamentally about ideology (capitalism vs. communism). Without that "why," you have a list, not an analysis.

Stage 4 — Think Like a Historian: Argue

Now you analyze, evaluate theses, and practice the exact skills that earn points on SAQs, LEQs, and DBQs — causation, CCOT, thesis writing, and contextualization.

4A · Causal Chain — Fill in each connecting explanation
📌 AP Skill: Causation For each arrow, write 1–2 sentences explaining how and why one event caused the next. Use specific evidence from the video.
🔫 1914 — Assassination of Archduke Franz Ferdinand, Sarajevo
↓ How did this trigger a world war?
⚔️ World War I (1914–1918) — The "War to End All Wars"
↓ How did WWI's strain cause revolution in Russia?
🔴 1917 — Russian Revolution; Bolsheviks seize power
↓ Why was the 1919 peace "fragile and flawed"?
📜 1919 — Treaty of Versailles: Fragile, punishing peace
↓ How did economic collapse enable a second war?
💥 1929 → Rise of Fascism → World War II
✓ Sample Causal Chain
Assassination → WWI: The assassination of Franz Ferdinand triggered Austria-Hungary's ultimatum to Serbia. Because of the alliance system, Serbia's rejection pulled in Russia (Serbia's ally), which triggered Germany (Austria's ally) to declare war on Russia and France, and then Britain entered when Germany invaded Belgium. In six weeks, a Balkan crisis became a world war.

WWI → Russian Revolution: Three years of devastating total war destroyed Russia's economy and military morale — food shortages at home, massive casualties at the front. The tsar's government lost all legitimacy, creating the perfect conditions for the Bolshevik takeover in October 1917.

WWI → Fragile Peace: The Versailles Treaty assigned Germany sole "war guilt," imposed crushing reparations, stripped territory, and limited its military — but it failed to create genuine reconciliation or address the imperial and nationalist rivalries that had caused the war in the first place.

Fragile Peace → WWII: The Great Depression (1929) destroyed Germany's already shattered economy, making millions desperate and resentful. This allowed the Nazi Party to exploit Versailles grievances, promising national revival. Without a strong response from Western democracies, Germany rearmed and expanded, and a second, even more devastating war became inevitable.
4B · SAQ Targeted Drill — "Explain" (1 point)
📌 SAQ Format This mirrors an SAQ Part B or C "explain" prompt. Write 2–3 sentences. Show how or why — not just what. Use specific evidence.
Explain Explain how mass atrocities in the twentieth century represent a categorically different form of state violence compared to ordinary warfare.
Use at least one specific historical example. 2–3 sentences. This is worth 1 SAQ point.
✓ Sample Answer (earns the point)
Mass atrocities were categorically different from ordinary warfare because they involved deliberate, state-sponsored policies targeting civilian populations based on identity — not military strategy or necessity. The Holocaust, for example, was not collateral damage from battle but a systematic government program of extermination designed and administered by the Nazi state, using industrial methods to murder six million Jews and millions of others. Similarly, the Armenian Genocide during WWI was a calculated campaign by the Ottoman government, demonstrating that these atrocities reflected ideological intent — the elimination of a perceived enemy within — rather than the tragic accidents of combat.
Why this earns the point: Directly explains how and why atrocities differ — through deliberate state policy targeting identity, not military opponents. Uses two specific examples (Holocaust, Armenian Genocide) and ties them to the core argument. This is far stronger than simply saying "more people died."
4C · Continuity & Change Over Time (CCOT)
Explain Using evidence from the video, identify and explain one significant continuity AND one significant change in the nature of warfare between 1900 and 1945.
Write 2–3 sentences for each. Include a brief explanation of why it continued or changed.
ONE CONTINUITY:
ONE CHANGE:
✓ Sample Answer
Continuity: Great power competition and imperial rivalry persisted throughout the period. The same nations that had competed for colonial empires before 1900 remained locked in conflict through 1945, suggesting that the underlying structure of international competition — multiple powerful states competing for dominance — remained fundamentally unchanged even as the specific ideologies and weapons evolved.

Change: The targeting and scale of warfare changed dramatically. Before 1900, warfare primarily involved professional armies on defined battlefields with limited civilian impact. By WWII, total war had eliminated the distinction between soldier and civilian — industrial bombing campaigns targeted cities, and the atomic bomb demonstrated that a single weapon could destroy an entire urban population in seconds, fundamentally transforming the relationship between states and the use of violence.
4D · Spot the Thesis — Click each statement to evaluate it
📌 Thesis Standard A thesis must: (1) make a historically defensible claim, (2) establish a line of reasoning — not just state a fact or restate the prompt.

Prompt: "Evaluate the extent to which the consequences of World War I caused World War II."

"The consequences of World War I caused World War II."
✗ Does NOT earn the pointThis is a direct restatement of the prompt. It makes no claim about degree or mechanism, offers no line of reasoning, and gives a reader no sense of what argument the essay will make.
"World War I had many important consequences that eventually led to World War II in very significant ways."
✗ Does NOT earn the pointVague and circular — "important" and "very significant" carry no historical content. No line of reasoning is established. A reader still doesn't know what argument will follow.
"Although economic instability and the rise of fascist ideology also contributed to the outbreak of World War II, the punitive terms of the Treaty of Versailles were the most significant cause because they simultaneously destroyed Germany's economy, delegitimized its democratic government, and fueled the nationalist resentment that fascist movements needed to seize power."
✓ EARNS the pointThis thesis acknowledges complexity (other causes existed), makes a specific claim about degree ("most significant"), and establishes a three-part line of reasoning (economic destruction, delegitimized democracy, nationalist resentment). A reader knows exactly what the essay will argue and why.
"While WWI's aftermath laid the structural foundations for WWII, the ideological rise of fascism was equally important, as fascist movements actively exploited post-war grievances to build mass support that democratic institutions — already weakened by economic crisis — proved too fragile to contain."
✓ EARNS the pointTakes a nuanced position (shared causation) rather than single-cause determinism. Establishes a clear line of reasoning: fascism exploited post-war structural weaknesses, and democracy failed to contain it. Historically defensible and analytically specific.
4E · Contextualization Paragraph Builder
📌 Contextualization — What earns the point Describe a broader historical context (before or surrounding the period) that is meaningfully connected to the prompt. Must be 3–5 sentences — not just a phrase. Must explicitly connect to the topic.

Choose a context topic and write your paragraph below:

✓ Sample Contextualization Paragraph (earns the point)
By the late nineteenth century, European great powers had transformed imperial competition into a defining feature of their national identities and economic systems. The scramble for colonies across Africa and Asia generated enormous wealth for colonial powers but also produced intense rivalry, as nations like Germany — arriving late to the imperial race — felt excluded from a global order dominated by Britain and France. This competition directly fueled an arms race, as governments invested in massive military buildups to project power and defend their overseas empires. The uneven distribution of colonial territory created festering resentments that made diplomatic solutions nearly impossible when crises arose. It was precisely this pre-existing structure of imperial rivalry and militarized competition that transformed a regional assassination in 1914 into a global catastrophe, and the failure to address these rivalries in the peace settlement only guaranteed that they would resurface.
Why this earns the point: Describes broader context (imperial competition) that precedes the period, uses 5 sentences, and explicitly connects to the prompt in the final sentence. It is clearly about something larger than the events of the prompt itself.

5A · 15 AP-Style MCQ Questions
1 of 15
Which of the following best describes the systemic reason why the assassination of Archduke Franz Ferdinand escalated into a world war?
Explanation
Answer B. The alliance system meant that when Austria-Hungary declared war on Serbia, Russia mobilized to defend Serbia, which triggered Germany to declare war on Russia and France, which triggered Britain to enter when Germany invaded Belgium. The systemic (structural) cause was that no single government could de-escalate once the mechanism started.
2 of 15
A historian argues that "total war fundamentally transformed the relationship between governments and their citizens." Which of the following best supports this argument?
Explanation
Answer B. Total war required the state to penetrate domestic society in unprecedented ways — conscription, rationing, factory control, mass propaganda. This expanded government power fundamentally changed the nature of governance itself.
3 of 15
The Treaty of Versailles' "war guilt" clause (Article 231) is historically significant primarily because it:
Explanation
Answer C. The war guilt clause was the legal foundation for reparations — Germany couldn't argue against paying if it had "admitted" causing the war. This double burden (moral humiliation + financial ruin) made the Weimar Republic deeply unpopular with its own citizens.
4 of 15
Which historical thinking skill is most directly demonstrated by the "domino effect" timeline in the video (1914 assassination → 1917 revolution → 1919 treaty → 1929 depression → WWII)?
Explanation
Answer C. Causation is the skill of explaining why events happened (causes) and what resulted from them (effects). The domino timeline is a perfect illustration of multi-step causation — each event was caused by the previous one and caused the next.
5 of 15
The video's comparison between Western democracies and the Soviet Union's responses to the Great Depression is primarily an example of which AP historical thinking skill?
Explanation
Answer B. Any time a prompt asks you to explain why two things were similar or different, you are doing historical comparison. The key is always to explain the reason for the difference — here, the ideological difference between capitalism and communism.
6 of 15
Which of the following best explains why WWI is considered a turning point in the history of warfare?
Explanation
Answer C. The combination of industrial-scale weaponry and total war mobilization is what made WWI historically significant as a turning point. Previous wars had professional armies; WWI mobilized entire societies and used technology to kill at industrial scale.
7 of 15
A student writes: "The Armenian Genocide, the Holocaust, and Stalin's purges are all examples of mass atrocities because they involved very large numbers of deaths." What is the most significant problem with this analysis?
Explanation
Answer B. On the AP exam, strong analysis requires identifying the correct characteristic, not just a correlated one. Mass atrocities are defined by intent (deliberate state policy) and targeting (based on identity), not simply by body count. Many battles killed enormous numbers of people without being mass atrocities.
8 of 15
Which thesis statement would BEST earn the thesis point on an LEQ asking: "Evaluate the extent to which imperialism caused the outbreak of World War I"?
Explanation
Answer C. The thesis point requires a defensible claim AND a line of reasoning. Option C does both: it takes a position on extent (essential precondition but not sufficient alone) and explains the mechanism (imperialism created conditions that nationalism and alliances then weaponized). This gives the reader a roadmap for the essay.
9 of 15
The video describes WWII as "the horrifying peak of industrial warfare." Which development most clearly supports this characterization?
Explanation
Answer D. The atomic bomb represents the logical endpoint of industrial warfare — the maximum application of industrial technology to destruction. The video explicitly says it changed the relationship between humanity and war "forever" — making it the defining marker of the "peak."
10 of 15
A contextualization paragraph for an essay on WWI begins: "The Industrial Revolution transformed European societies in the 19th century by creating new factory-based economies and urban working classes." To earn the contextualization point, what must the paragraph do next?
Explanation
Answer B. The College Board rubric requires that contextualization must be connected to the prompt — not just described. A common student mistake is writing 5 excellent sentences of historical context that never explicitly link to the prompt. You must complete the connection.
11 of 15
Which of the following is a continuity (something that stayed the same) in the era of global conflict from 1900–1945?
Explanation
Answer C. On CCOT questions, always ask: what structural feature persisted even as the surface changed? Great power competition is the structural continuity — the specific empires, ideologies, and weapons changed dramatically, but the underlying competition for dominance between major states persisted throughout.
12 of 15
The video argues that the era of global conflict (1900–1945) was shaped by a tension between "what stayed the same and what was brand new." Which pairing best illustrates this tension?
Explanation
Answer B. CCOT analysis requires identifying something old (continuity) alongside something genuinely new (change). Imperial rivalry had characterized European power politics for centuries — communism was a radical new ideology that challenged the entire established order. This is the core CCOT tension the video describes.
13 of 15
The video says that after WWI, Western democracies and the Soviet Union responded to "the same global problems" in "radically different ways." For an SAQ asking you to describe this difference, which response would most clearly earn the point?
Explanation
Answer C. The AP "describe" standard requires relevant characteristics — not just naming something. Option C describes what each system actually did and why they differed. This is the standard that separates a 0 from a 1 on SAQ "describe" prompts.
14 of 15
Which of the following demonstrates the most sophisticated historical argumentation about the causes of WWII?
Explanation
Answer D. Sophisticated argumentation means: acknowledging multiple factors, explaining their interaction (how one enabled another), assigning relative significance (which mattered more, in what way), and using precise language ("structural conditions" vs. "ideological radicalism"). Option D does all of this; the others are simpler claims.
15 of 15
The video ends by saying the goal of Unit 7 is to "understand why it happened," not just what happened. In AP World History terms, this emphasis on "why" corresponds most directly to which skill?
Explanation
Answer D. "Why did this happen?" is the causation question. Memorizing dates and events is the "what" — understanding the mechanisms, conditions, and forces that drove those events is the "why." Causation is the skill that separates narrative history from analytical history, and it is central to every AP free-response question.
