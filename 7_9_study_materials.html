🔍 The Two Suspects — Causes of 20th-Century Global Conflict

The video organizes the AP curriculum's causes into two main categories. Learn both deeply — the exam will ask you to weigh them, not just list them.

⚙️Suspect 1 — Scientific & Technological Change
  • Deadlier weapons: Machine guns, poison gas, tanks, aerial bombing, atomic bomb — technology transformed what a battlefield could do
  • Mass propaganda enabled: Industrial printing, radio, film allowed governments to mobilize entire populations for war — technology is what made total war possible
  • Global economy reshaped: Industrial technology fueled imperial rivalries as powers competed for resources, markets, and strategic position
  • Escalation logic: Total mobilization of science produced ever-more-destructive weapons in a self-reinforcing cycle
  • Civilian deaths: WWII sees 20 million civilians dead in USSR, 15 million in China — a direct result of technologies that erased the soldier/civilian line
⚑Suspect 2 — Political & Social Challenges
  • Imperial collapse: Ottoman, Russian, Qing empires collapse → power vacuums, ethnic conflicts, new unstable states. 1.5M Indian soldiers in WWI — empires literally drag the world into war
  • Explosive nationalism: New nation-states defined by ethnicity create minority problems; nationalism celebrates conflict as proof of national vitality
  • Institutional failure: League of Nations proves toothless; appeasement fails; collective security collapses — no system to enforce peace
  • Extreme ideologies: Fascism, Nazism, Stalinism don't just accept war — they celebrate or require it as the engine of national/class destiny
  • Internal instability: Mexican Revolution, Russian Revolution — internal social pressures explode into violence, part of a global pattern of old-order breakdown

The League of Nations failed to stop Japan in Manchuria (1931) or Italy in Ethiopia (1935), exposing collective security as a paper tiger. The failure to enforce international order sent a message: aggression would go unpunished. This was a political failure, not a technological one.

⚖️ The Three-Question Checklist for Weighing Significance

From the Video — Use This on Every AP Essay

1
Deep cause or surface trigger? Ask: was this a deep underlying cause that had been building for years (like imperialism, nationalism, or industrialization) — or a surface-level trigger that ignited an already-loaded situation (like the assassination of Franz Ferdinand)? Deep causes are more historically significant than triggers.
2
One of many, or the tipping point? Was this cause one of several factors with roughly equal impact — or was it the factor that, when added, made conflict unavoidable? The "tipping point" cause is more significant than background conditions.
3
First domino? Trace the chain of cause and effect. Was this the cause that set off all the others in sequence? A cause that generates other causes is more significant than one that stands alone. Ask: what would have been different if this cause had been removed?

📐 Model Argument Analysis — Weak vs. Strong

The video contrasts a weak argument with a strong one. Study both — then explain in your own words what makes the strong argument better.

✗ WEAK ARGUMENT

"World War I was caused by many factors, including technology, nationalism, imperialism, and the alliance system. All of these played an important role in starting the conflict."

What's wrong: This is a list, not an argument. It identifies multiple causes but makes no claim about their relative significance. It doesn't argue which mattered more, how they interacted, or what the chain of causation was. No line of reasoning, no analytical claim. This earns zero thesis/complexity points on the AP exam.
✓ STRONG ARGUMENT (from the video)

"While new weapons made World War I more destructive, it was the political factors — the collapse of the old imperial order and the catastrophic failure of the alliance system — that made the war inevitable in the first place."

Why it works: Acknowledges technology's role (nuance), then makes a stronger comparative claim (political factors made it inevitable vs. technology made it more destructive). These are different claims about different causal roles — more destructive ≠ more inevitable. That distinction is the argument. This earns thesis and potentially complexity points.
Explain Prompt

In your own words, explain what makes the strong argument above better than the weak one. What specific technique is it using, and why does the AP exam reward that technique?

👁 Reveal Sample Explanation ▼
SAMPLE — DOK 2

The strong argument uses a specific technique the video calls "nuance" — it doesn't simply pick one side and ignore the other. Instead, it acknowledges what the other side contributed ("new weapons made the war more destructive") while still making a stronger claim about relative significance ("it was the political factors that made it inevitable"). This is crucial because it shows the writer understands both causes while still making an argument about which mattered more.

The technique being used is distinguishing between different types of causal roles: "more destructive" (technology's contribution) versus "inevitable" (political factors' contribution) are not competing claims about the same thing — they are claims about different dimensions of causation. Technology escalated the war's destruction; political and institutional failures made the war impossible to avoid. By making this distinction explicit, the argument demonstrates the kind of analytical precision the AP exam rewards with complexity points.

The AP exam rewards this because historical causation is rarely a matter of one cause versus another — it's usually a question of which cause did what kind of work. The complexity point specifically asks students to "explain multiple causes or effects" and to demonstrate "nuanced understanding." An argument that distinguishes between causes' different roles (necessary vs. sufficient, underlying vs. triggering, escalating vs. inevitable) earns that complexity credit in a way that a list never can.

✍️ Explain Drill 1 — The Role of Imperial Collapse
Explain Prompt

The video describes imperial collapse as one of the most significant political causes of 20th-century global conflict. Using at least two specific examples from Unit 7, explain how the collapse of land-based and maritime empires created the conditions that made large-scale conflict more likely.

👁 Reveal Sample Answer ▼
SAMPLE ANSWER — DOK 2

Imperial collapse contributed to 20th-century global conflict through two distinct mechanisms. First, the collapse of the great land-based empires created dangerous power vacuums that destabilized entire regions. The fall of the Russian Empire in 1917 — directly caused by the strains of WWI — paved the way for the Bolshevik Revolution and the rise of Soviet communism, an ideology that would define global conflict for the next 50 years. The collapse of the Ottoman Empire left behind contested territories with mixed ethnic populations and competing nationalist movements, creating the unstable conditions that enabled the Armenian genocide and contributed to decades of Middle Eastern conflict. When empires disappeared from the map, the governance structures that had managed ethnic and political diversity disappeared with them.

Second, the maritime empires connected distant regions in ways that turned European conflicts into world wars. The British Empire alone contributed 1.5 million Indian soldiers, over 400,000 Canadians, and over 300,000 Australians to WWI. These soldiers didn't choose to fight in a conflict that began in the Balkans — they were mobilized by imperial obligation. This is the video's key insight about imperial scale: empires didn't just create conflicts, they globalized them. What might have remained a European war became a world war precisely because imperial networks stretched across the globe. The collapse of that system didn't make conflict less likely; it made it more chaotic and more far-reaching.

✍️ Explain Drill 2 — Technology as Cause, Not Just Effect
Explain Prompt

The video argues that technology was "way more complex than just a neutral tool" — it didn't just shape how wars were fought, it contributed to making them happen. Explain two ways that technological change acted as a cause of 20th-century conflict, not merely a consequence of it.

👁 Reveal Sample Answer ▼
SAMPLE ANSWER — DOK 2

Technology's causal role in 20th-century conflict goes well beyond making weapons deadlier. First, industrial technology fundamentally reshaped the global economy in ways that intensified imperial competition. By the late 19th and early 20th centuries, industrial powers needed raw materials, markets, and strategic resources at global scale. This economic logic drove the "Scramble for Africa," the competition for Asian markets, and the naval arms race — all of which built up the tensions that made WWI's outbreak more likely. Technology didn't just determine how wars were fought; it determined why great powers wanted to control the territories that eventually became flashpoints.

Second, technology made mass propaganda possible for the first time in history. Industrial printing, photography, radio, and film allowed governments to reach and mobilize entire populations — creating the cultural and psychological conditions for total war. Without this technology, governments could not have sustained the public support needed to fight wars lasting years. Propaganda technology also enabled the spread of extremist ideologies at mass scale — Nazi radio broadcasts, Soviet state media, and wartime censorship all used industrial communication technology to shape what entire populations believed about their enemies. In this sense, technology was a cause of the war's scale and duration, not merely a tool that made it more destructive.

DOK 3 Knowledge Analysis
📝 Short Answer Question (SAQ)
SAQ Claim

"The unprecedented scale of violence in the 20th century resulted from the interaction of technological change and political instability, neither of which alone was sufficient to cause global conflict."

AIdentify ONE specific example of how technological change contributed to the scale or outbreak of 20th-century global conflict.
BExplain ONE way that political or social challenges — such as imperial collapse, nationalism, institutional failure, or extreme ideology — contributed to 20th-century global conflict in ways that technology alone cannot explain.
CExplain how ONE development from Unit 7 demonstrates the interaction between technological change and political/social challenges — showing that both were necessary to produce the outcome.
👁 Reveal Full SAQ Sample Response ▼
SAMPLE SAQ — DOK 3

Part A: The development and use of the atomic bomb represents the clearest example of how technological change contributed to the scale of 20th-century conflict. The Manhattan Project (1942–1945) mobilized 130,000+ workers and $2 billion to create a weapon capable of destroying an entire city in a single strike. When deployed at Hiroshima (August 6, 1945), it killed approximately 140,000 people — a scale of destruction no previous weapon could achieve in a single act. Technology directly enabled a qualitative escalation in the capacity for violence that reshaped not only WWII's end but the entire subsequent logic of Cold War deterrence.

Part B: The failure of the League of Nations to prevent Japanese aggression in Manchuria (1931) or Italian aggression in Ethiopia (1935) demonstrates a form of causation that technology cannot explain. The League failed not because of any technological limitation but because of a political design flaw: great powers retained national sovereignty over their foreign policy, and the League lacked enforcement mechanisms with real teeth. The US never even joined. When Japan faced no serious consequences for its Manchurian conquest, it sent a message that aggressive powers could act without punishment — a direct political cause of the escalating aggression that led to WWII. No weapon or industrial capacity made this failure inevitable; it was a failure of political will and institutional design.

Part C: The Holocaust demonstrates most powerfully that technology and political factors were both necessary and neither alone sufficient. The Holocaust required Nazi racial ideology (political) to identify the target group and provide ideological justification; it required the full apparatus of state power (political) to organize and enforce the genocide; and it required modern industrial technology — rail networks, census data systems, industrial gas chambers — to execute murder at the scale of six million people. Remove the ideology: no Holocaust, just prejudice. Remove the state power: no Holocaust, just persecution. Remove the industrial technology: no Holocaust at anything approaching that scale. The worst outcome of the 20th century required the terrible synergy of all three — which is precisely why the video insists that you cannot just list the suspects. You have to show how they worked together.

🎓 AP Exam Insight — Causation Arguments

For AP causation essays, the most common mistake is treating the prompt as a true/false question: "Was technology the most significant cause? Yes/No." The strongest arguments treat it as a weighing question: "In what ways was technology significant, and how does that compare to political/social factors?" Arguments that acknowledge complexity — "while technology X, it was political factor Y that made Z possible" — earn both the thesis point and potentially complexity points. The video's model argument shows exactly this structure.

LEQ Prompt

👁 Reveal Full Sample LEQ Response ▼
SAMPLE FULL LEQ — DOK 4

Contextualization: The Industrial Revolution (c. 1750–1900) fundamentally transformed both the scale of military power and the intensity of imperial competition among European states. By the late 19th century, industrial technology had enabled the mass production of weapons, the construction of global naval empires, and the extraction of resources from colonies across Africa and Asia at unprecedented scale. This industrial competition created a system of overlapping imperial rivalries, arms races, and alliance networks — a powder keg that by 1914 required only a spark to ignite. The technological transformations of the Industrial Revolution thus created the structural preconditions for 20th-century global conflict, even as the specific triggers of those conflicts were overwhelmingly political in character.

Thesis: While scientific and technological change dramatically escalated the scale of destruction in 20th-century global conflict, the political and social challenges of the era — particularly the collapse of empires, the failure of international institutions, and the rise of extreme ideologies — were ultimately more significant causes, because they determined not just how destructively wars were fought but whether they occurred at all.

Body Paragraph 1 — Technology's Real Role: Technology shaped 20th-century conflict in ways that go beyond simply making weapons more lethal. Industrial technology enabled mass propaganda — radio, film, and print allowed governments to mobilize entire populations for total war in ways impossible in previous centuries. Without this technology, democratic governments could not have sustained the public support needed for years-long conflicts, and totalitarian states could not have built the ideological consensus required for genocide. Most dramatically, the atomic bomb — the product of the Manhattan Project's 130,000+ workers — demonstrated that total mobilization of science could produce weapons capable of destroying entire cities in a single strike. By the end of WWII, technological development had killed approximately 20 million Soviet civilians and 15 million Chinese civilians, numbers that reflect the direct lethal impact of aerial bombing, firebombing, and nuclear weapons. Technology thus deserves significant credit as a cause of the wars' scale.

Body Paragraph 2 — Political Causes as More Fundamental: However, technology cannot explain why these conflicts began in the first place. The failure of the League of Nations to stop Japanese aggression in Manchuria (1931) or Italian aggression in Ethiopia (1935) was a purely political failure — a matter of institutional design and political will, not technological capacity. The appeasement of Hitler at Munich (1938) reflected political miscalculation, not technological limitation. Most fundamentally, the Versailles Treaty's punitive terms (1919) planted the seeds of WWII by humiliating Germany and creating the political instability that Hitler's movement exploited — no amount of technological change can explain why democratic governments made these political choices. The video's three-question checklist clarifies the distinction: technology was a deep cause that amplified destruction, but the political cascade — imperial collapse creating power vacuums, nationalism defining enemies, institutional failures enabling aggression — was the sequence of first dominoes that made global conflict inevitable. Technology made WWI more destructive; political and institutional failures made WWII inevitable.

Complexity — Interaction and Cross-Period Connection: The most honest assessment recognizes that technology and political factors were deeply intertwined: the Holocaust required both Nazi racial ideology (political) and industrial technology to operate death camps at scale (technological). Neither alone was sufficient. This interaction is also visible in the Cold War that followed: the atomic bomb — a technological development — directly shaped the political doctrine of Mutually Assured Destruction, creating a new political framework entirely structured around technological capacity. The causation argument from 7.9 thus extends into Unit 8: the political consequences of 20th-century technology continued to generate global conflict for decades after 1945, in a pattern where technology and political structures kept reshaping each other.

👁 Reveal Sample Synthesis Response ▼
SAMPLE SYNTHESIS — DOK 4

Applying the 7.9 framework to the Cold War reveals both continuity and a significant shift in the balance between the two suspects. Technology — specifically nuclear weapons — played an even more central role in Cold War conflict than in the world wars, because the very existence of atomic arsenals created a new political logic: Mutually Assured Destruction. The nuclear balance of terror structured every aspect of Cold War geopolitics. In this sense, technology was a deeper and more autonomous cause in the Cold War than it was in the world wars, where technology amplified political conflicts but didn't determine their fundamental structure.

However, the political/social suspect remained equally important — it just operated differently. Cold War conflict was driven by ideological competition between capitalism and communism (political/social), not by imperial territorial rivalries. Proxy wars in Korea, Vietnam, Angola, and Afghanistan were fought for political and ideological stakes, with technology providing the weapons but ideology providing the stakes. Decolonization — the political consequence of imperial collapse that Unit 7 began analyzing — created the unstable new states where Cold War competition played out.

The key shift: in the world wars, technology enabled political conflicts to escalate to catastrophic scale. In the Cold War, technology (nuclear weapons) simultaneously prevented direct great-power conflict (deterrence) while the political/ideological struggle continued through proxy wars. The two suspects didn't just continue — they entered a new kind of interaction, where technological capacity both enabled and constrained political conflict in ways that would have been unrecognizable in 1914. The 7.9 framework holds, but the relationship between the suspects changed fundamentally after Hiroshima.
